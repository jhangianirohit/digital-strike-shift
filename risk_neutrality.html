Risk-Neutral Pricing

A single example that explains why derivatives pricing works

1. The Setup

Consider a simple stock with two possible outcomes tomorrow:

Today: $100
60% probability
Up: $120
40% probability
Down: $90

The risk-free rate is 5%. You can lend or borrow at this rate.

2. The Question

Why is the stock priced at $100 today?

If we compute the expected value:

E[S] = 0.60 × $120 + 0.40 × $90 = $108

Discounted at the risk-free rate: $108 / 1.05 = $102.86

So why isn't the stock trading at $102.86?

3. The Answer: Risk Aversion

Investors are risk-averse. They won't pay the full expected value for an uncertain payoff.

They demand a risk premium — extra expected return — to compensate for bearing uncertainty.

Expected return = $108 / $100 - 1 = 8% Risk-free rate = 5% ───────────────────────────────────────── Equity risk premium = 3%

The stock is "cheap" relative to expected value because investors require that 3% premium to hold it.

4. Two Ways to See the Same Price

There are two equivalent ways to arrive at the $100 price:

Real World (P-measure)

Probabilities: 60% up, 40% down

Expected payoff: $108

Discount rate: 8% (includes risk premium)

$108 / 1.08 = $100

Risk-Neutral (Q-measure)

Probabilities: 50% up, 50% down

Expected payoff: $105

Discount rate: 5% (risk-free)

$105 / 1.05 = $100

Same price, different framing. P puts the risk premium in the discount rate; Q puts it in the probabilities.

5. Finding the Risk-Neutral Probabilities

Under Q, the expected return must equal the risk-free rate:

EQ[S] = S₀ × (1 + r) EQ[S] = $100 × 1.05 = $105 Let q = probability of up-state under Q: q × $120 + (1-q) × $90 = $105 30q = 15 q = 0.50
State P (Real World) Q (Risk-Neutral)
Up → $120 60% 50%
Down → $90 40% 50%

Q downweights the good outcome and upweights the bad outcome — that's how it removes the risk premium.

6. Why Risk-Neutral Pricing Matters

Now suppose we want to price a call option with strike $100.

Payoffs: Up state: max($120 - $100, 0) = $20 Down state: max($90 - $100, 0) = $0

Under P

E[payoff] = 0.60 × $20 = $12

But what discount rate? 8%? Higher? We don't know!

The option has different risk than the stock.

Under Q

EQ[payoff] = 0.50 × $20 = $10

Discount at risk-free rate:

$10 / 1.05 = $9.52

Done. No risk premium needed.

The Key Insight

Under Q, we always discount at the risk-free rate. We don't need to figure out the "right" risk premium for each derivative — the probability adjustment handles it.

7. Why Does This Work?

The Replication Argument

The call option can be replicated using the stock and borrowing:

Portfolio: Buy Δ shares, borrow $B at 5% Up state: Δ × $120 - B × 1.05 = $20 (match call payoff) Down state: Δ × $90 - B × 1.05 = $0 (match call payoff) Solving: Δ = ($20 - $0) / ($120 - $90) = 0.667 shares B = (Δ × $90) / 1.05 = $57.14 Cost today: 0.667 × $100 - $57.14 = $9.52

The replicating portfolio costs exactly what Q-pricing gives us. This is not a coincidence — it's the foundation of derivatives pricing.

No Arbitrage

If the option traded at any other price:

  • Price > $9.52 → Sell the option, buy the replicating portfolio → risk-free profit
  • Price < $9.52 → Buy the option, sell the replicating portfolio → risk-free profit

Arbitrageurs enforce that option price = replication cost = Q-price.

8. The Two Measures: Summary

P (Real World) Q (Risk-Neutral)
Probabilities Actual/subjective Adjusted (no risk premium)
Expected return μ (includes risk premium) r (risk-free)
Used for Forecasting, portfolio allocation Derivatives pricing
Discount rate Risk-adjusted (varies by asset) Risk-free (always r)

9. Practical Implications

For Trading

  • Directional views don't make options "cheap" — the stock price already reflects expected returns
  • Valid option edges: volatility, skew, correlation — things Q doesn't capture well
  • Risk premia exist because real investors are risk-averse; you can harvest them if you can bear the variance

For Life

  • Insurance = paying the risk premium — you pay to transfer tail risk to someone else
  • Risky career choices = long equity risk premium on yourself
  • The premium is the price of fear — someone risk-neutral would harvest it

The Takeaway

Risk-neutral pricing doesn't assume investors are risk-neutral.
It says: if you can hedge away the risk, risk preferences don't matter.

Q is a mathematical tool that lets us price derivatives
without knowing the market's risk aversion — because hedging eliminates it.

Reading List

To go deeper, in rough order of accessibility:

  • The Concepts and Practice of Mathematical Finance
    Mark Joshi
    Best introduction to derivatives pricing. Explains the intuition before the math. Chapters 1-6 cover everything here in depth.
  • Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives
    John Hull
    The standard textbook. Comprehensive but accessible. Good reference for specific products.
  • Dynamic Hedging
    Nassim Taleb
    Practical perspective from a trader. Emphasizes what Black-Scholes gets wrong in practice. Read after you understand the theory.
  • Paul Wilmott Introduces Quantitative Finance
    Paul Wilmott
    Intuitive and visual. Good at explaining the "why" behind the math. Two-volume set goes very deep.
  • Stochastic Calculus for Finance (Volumes I & II)
    Steven Shreve
    The rigorous treatment. Volume I covers discrete time (like our example). Volume II covers continuous time (Black-Scholes). For when you want the full mathematical machinery.
  • The Volatility Surface
    Jim Gatheral
    Advanced. What happens when Black-Scholes assumptions break down. Stochastic vol, local vol, the smile. Read after Shreve.
